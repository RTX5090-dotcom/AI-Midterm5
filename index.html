Section 01 — About the Project

Project Overview

Project Title:
Smarter Seating: Using AI to Redesign Rest Areas in St. Nicholas Park

Goal:
To identify where seating is missing in St. Nicholas Park and use AI tools to propose an optimal, community-centered bench and rest area redesign that improves comfort, accessibility, and daily use for every visitor.

Essential Question:
How can artificial intelligence help us identify where people need rest areas most in an urban park — and what does a smarter, more equitable seating solution look like?

Track: Urban Park Redesign — AI for Community Infrastructure

Section 02 — The Problem

Problem Definition

St. Nicholas Park stretches 23 acres across 13 city blocks — but its benches are almost entirely concentrated along the main walkway near the 135th Street entrance. The hillside interior, upper park, dog run, and playground areas have little to no seating. The last major bench installation was nearly 30 years ago.

👴
Elderly Visitors Cannot complete the full length of the park without a rest point, effectively cutting them off from most of the space.
👥
Parents & Caregivers Forced to stand while supervising children at the playground because no seating exists nearby.
♿
People with Disabilities Excluded from interior and hillside areas entirely — no ADA rest points break up the accessible routes.
🕐
Every Visitor Leaves earlier than they would like, reducing community presence and overall engagement with the park.

Section 03 — Site & Context

Site Understanding

A site analysis of St. Nicholas Park reveals clear patterns in how visitors move, rest, and experience the space — and where the absence of seating creates the greatest daily burden.

 Movement Patterns

Most visitors enter from the 135th and 141st Street gates and cluster near the courts and lower path. Few venture uphill — likely because there is nowhere to stop and rest along the steep incline.

 Comfort & Rest

The park slopes sharply from west to east. Climbing takes real effort, yet no benches exist at the top or anywhere along the ascent — making a mid-walk rest completely impossible.

 Accessibility

ADA paths exist near main entrances but no rest points break up the long accessible routes through the interior. Wheelchair users and elderly visitors have nowhere to pause.

 Community Need

The surrounding Harlem neighborhood has a high senior population and many families with young children — the two groups most harmed by the lack of rest infrastructure.

Section 04 — Design Options

Three AI-Generated Proposals

Using AI analysis of foot traffic patterns, accessibility routes, shade coverage, and community demographics, three seating redesign proposals were developed. Each takes a different approach to solving the rest area problem at St. Nicholas Park.

Option A

Bench Cluster Nodes

Place grouped bench clusters at 4 high-traffic nodes identified by AI heat mapping: near the dog run, playground, basketball courts, and hilltop overlook. Cost-effective but leaves most of the park uncovered.

  •  Low cost
  •  Quick to install
  •  Limited coverage
  •  No smart monitoring
✓ Selected Proposal Option B

Smart Solar Bench Network

AI-optimized placement of 18 solar-powered smart benches distributed across the full park based on foot traffic data, shade analysis, and accessibility mapping — not guesswork. Includes USB charging and real-time alerts.

  •  Full park coverage
  •  Solar powered
  •  Data-driven placement
  •  ADA compliant options
Option C

Shade Pavilions

Build 3 open-air shade pavilions at the park’s largest open areas. Maximum comfort and rain cover, but requires permits, structural construction, and a budget far higher than the other options.

  •  Maximum comfort
  •  Rain & shade cover
  •  Very high cost
  •  Requires permits

Section 05 — Final Proposal

Smart Solar Bench Network

The selected proposal uses AI-driven analysis to place 18 solar-powered smart benches at data-identified locations across the full length of St. Nicholas Park — prioritizing underserved hillside paths, the interior, and zones near existing amenities that currently have zero seating.

AI Foot Traffic Analysis

Computer vision and sensor data pinpoint which areas see heavy visitor use but have zero seating — placement based on real behavior, not assumptions.

Smart Placement Algorithm

AI scores every possible bench location using shade coverage, slope grade, accessibility proximity, and distance from other rest points to find the best 18 spots.

Solar-Powered & Self-Reporting

Each bench runs entirely on solar power and wirelessly sends usage data and maintenance alerts to NYC Parks in real time, reducing repair delays.

Accessibility First

6 of the 18 benches are ADA-compliant with armrests, extra width, and flat paved placement — specifically serving elderly visitors and people with mobility challenges.

Why this proposal? It combines data-driven precision with long-term sustainability and the lowest cost-to-impact ratio of all three options — making it the most realistic proposal for NYC Parks to actually fund and implement.

Section 06 — Reflection & Conclusion

Human Reflection on AI Use

This project used AI tools at every stage — from research and image generation to spatial analysis and proposal writing. But AI could not do everything. Here is an honest breakdown of what AI contributed and what required human judgment.

 What AI Helped With

  • Generating realistic images of current park conditions and future proposals
  • Analyzing foot traffic patterns to identify where seating is most needed
  • Scoring potential bench locations across multiple variables at once
  • Researching the park’s history, demographics, and past renovation records
  • Producing three distinct design proposals quickly for comparison
  • Drafting written content and justifications for each design option

 What Required Human Judgment

  • Deciding which problem to study — AI cannot feel what it is like to stand in a park with nowhere to sit
  • Choosing the final proposal based on community values, not just data scores
  • Recognizing that elderly and disabled residents are most affected — empathy AI cannot replicate
  • Evaluating which AI-generated images were accurate versus misleading
  • Prioritizing accessibility over aesthetics when the two conflicted
  • Writing this reflection — AI cannot genuinely evaluate its own role or limits

Conclusion

AI is powerful at processing data, spotting patterns, and generating options at a scale no individual could match alone. But the most important decisions in this project — who to design for, what values matter most, and why — were made by a human. The best park redesigns will always combine both.
